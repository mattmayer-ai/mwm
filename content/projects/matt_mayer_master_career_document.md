### World's First RAS Simulator

#### The Challenge
The Royal Canadian Navy faced a critical training challenge with Replenishment at Sea (RAS) operations. RAS involves two ships connecting via cables while moving to transfer fuel and supplies—a dangerous operation requiring precise coordination. Traditional training required actual ships at sea, costing $800,000 per day with limited training opportunities.

#### Innovation Approach
Matt reframed the problem: "How do we bring training to the crew instead of bringing the crew to training?" This led to developing the world's first integrated RAS simulator that could switch between operational and training modes.

#### Technical Specifications
- **Fidelity**: Millisecond-precision physics simulation
- **Integration**: Connected directly to ship's operational console
- **Modes**: Operational mode for real operations, training mode for practice
- **Portability**: Laptop-based version for training at sea
- **Scenarios**: 200+ training scenarios covering all weather conditions

#### Implementation Challenges

**Security Clearance Complexity**: The project required managing team members with different security clearance levels. Matt created information compartmentalization protocols allowing collaboration while maintaining security requirements.

**Technical Integration**: Integrating with actual ship systems required extensive coordination with multiple defense contractors. Matt orchestrated collaboration between Lockheed Martin (console manufacturer), L3Harris (ship systems), and the Navy's technical authority.

**Zero-Failure Requirement**: Military systems cannot fail in operation. Matt implemented triple redundancy in all critical systems and extensive testing protocols including 10,000+ hours of simulation testing.

#### Impact & Recognition
- **$20M+ annual operational savings** since 2019 deployment
- **90% reduction** in at-sea training requirements
- **Adopted by 3 allied navies** (Australia, UK, Netherlands)
- **Industry recognition**: Defense Innovation Award 2020

### Boeing VR Oxygen Mask Trainer

#### Project Origin
Boeing approached RaceRocks to solve a costly problem: improper oxygen mask installation in 737 MAX aircraft was causing delays and safety concerns. Each installation error cost $50,000 to fix and delayed aircraft delivery.

#### Initial Solution Design
Matt led development of a VR training system:
- Photorealistic 3D cockpit environment
- Hand tracking for precise movement
- Step-by-step installation guidance
- Error detection and correction feedback
- Performance analytics dashboard

#### The Beautiful Failure
Despite technical excellence, the project failed due to fundamental constraints:
- **Precision Gap**: 2018-era VR controllers couldn't replicate sub-centimeter finger movements required
- **Haptic Feedback**: Lack of tactile feedback made it impossible to "feel" proper seat of mask
- **Certification Requirements**: Boeing's training certification required physical manipulation

#### Lessons Learned
This failure became Matt's most valuable learning experience:
1. **Technology Timing**: Innovation alone isn't enough—need right timing
2. **Constraint Validation**: Always validate technical constraints before full development
3. **Stakeholder Alignment**: Ensure all stakeholders agree on success criteria upfront
4. **Graceful Failure**: Better to acknowledge limitations than ship unusable product

Matt now uses this case study when teaching product management, emphasizing that "the right product at the wrong time is still wrong."

#### Customer Journey Optimization Framework

**Moment of Intrigue vs. Aha Moment**:
Matt teaches students to distinguish between:
- Moment of Intrigue: When users understand potential value
- Aha Moment: When users are ready to commit

Most products fail by trying to jump straight to the aha moment without optimizing for intrigue first.

**Journey Optimization Process**:
1. Map current journey with all touchpoints
2. Identify friction points through user research
3. Prioritize by impact and effort
4. Design experiments to reduce friction
5. Measure impact on key metrics
6. Iterate based on learnings

#### The 70% Friction Reduction Method
Based on Air Canada success, Matt teaches:
1. Document every step in user journey
2. Time each step and identify delays
3. Count clicks, page loads, decisions required
4. Set target of 70% reduction
5. Prioritize highest-impact improvements
6. Validate improvements through A/B testing

### Conflict Resolution

#### Principled Approach
1. **Understand All Perspectives**: Listen to each party separately
2. **Find Common Ground**: Identify shared goals
3. **Focus on Interests, Not Positions**: Understand underlying needs
4. **Generate Options**: Brainstorm multiple solutions
5. **Use Objective Criteria**: Rely on data and principles
6. **Document Agreement**: Ensure clear understanding
